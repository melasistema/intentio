# Red Flags in Product Ideas

Not all product ideas are created equal. Here are warning signs that an idea may struggle, fail, or require significant pivoting.

## Problem Red Flags

### 1. The Problem Isn't Painful Enough
**Symptom**: People acknowledge the problem but aren't actively seeking solutions.

**Example**: "It would be nice to have a better way to organize my desk."  
**Why it fails**: Low pain = low willingness to change or pay.

**Test**: Ask "What have you tried to solve this?" If the answer is "nothing," the problem may not be severe enough.

---

### 2. The Problem Is Rare
**Symptom**: "This happened to me once..."

**Example**: A productivity app for people who work in noisy cafés exactly 2 days per week.  
**Why it fails**: Infrequent problems have low purchase urgency and poor retention.

**Threshold**: Problems should occur at least weekly for consumer products, daily for B2B tools.

---

### 3. The Problem Doesn't Generalize
**Symptom**: You're solving *your* problem, assuming others share it.

**Example**: "As a left-handed graphic designer who uses Linux, I need..."  
**Why it fails**: Hyper-specific problems limit market size.

**Test**: Interview 20 people in your target segment. If fewer than 12 resonate strongly, pivot.

---

### 4. The Problem Has Acceptable Workarounds
**Symptom**: People say "Yeah, but I just use [existing tool] for that."

**Example**: Building a new note-taking app when people use Google Docs, Notion, or physical notebooks successfully.  
**Why it fails**: Your solution must be **10x better** to overcome switching costs.

**Test**: Ask "What would make you switch from your current solution?" If the answer is vague, you're in trouble.

---

## Solution Red Flags

### 5. It's a Vitamin, Not a Painkiller
**Symptom**: The product is "nice to have" rather than urgent.

**Vitamins**: Preventative, optional, easy to forget (meditation apps, diet trackers)  
**Painkillers**: Immediate relief, urgent, high retention (messaging, payment processing)

**Why vitamins struggle**: Low urgency → low conversion → high churn.

**Mitigation**: If building a vitamin, focus on habit formation and integrate into daily workflows.

---

### 6. Complex Behavioral Change Required
**Symptom**: Your product requires users to completely change how they work.

**Example**: "First, you'll manually log every meal. Then invite your friends. Then connect your fitness tracker. Then..."  
**Why it fails**: Friction kills adoption. People resist change.

**Rule**: If your product requires more than 3 steps to show value, simplify ruthlessly.

---

### 7. The Solution Solves Part of a Workflow
**Symptom**: Your product is useful for 5 minutes, then users leave to complete the task elsewhere.

**Example**: A brainstorming tool that generates ideas but has no way to organize, share, or act on them.  
**Why it fails**: Partial solutions create friction. Users want end-to-end workflows.

**Test**: Map the user's full journey. If your product covers < 60%, consider expanding scope or integrating deeply into existing tools.

---

## Market Red Flags

### 8. Undifferentiated in a Crowded Market
**Symptom**: "It's like [successful product], but for [narrow niche]."

**Example**: "It's like Slack, but for dog walkers."  
**Why it fails**: Without clear differentiation, you're competing on price or marketing budget (which startups lose).

**Test**: Complete this sentence: "Unlike [competitor], we..." If you struggle, revisit your positioning.

---

### 9. Too Early or Too Late
**Symptom**: Market timing issues.

**Too early**: "Once everyone has VR headsets, this will be huge."  
**Too late**: "We're building a new social network to compete with Facebook."

**Why it fails**: Early = expensive customer education. Late = entrenched competitors.

**Test**: Is this a growing market with weak incumbents? That's the sweet spot.

---

### 10. Unclear Target Customer
**Symptom**: "It's for everyone" or "Anyone who wants to be more productive."

**Why it fails**: You can't market to everyone. You can't build features for everyone. You can't price for everyone.

**Rule**: If you can't name 10 specific people who'd use this tomorrow, your target is too broad.

---

## Business Model Red Flags

### 11. No Clear Path to Revenue
**Symptom**: "We'll figure out monetization later" or "We'll sell data/ads."

**Why it fails**: Monetization isn't an afterthought. It shapes product design, user acquisition, and sustainability.

**Test**: Can you articulate—today—who pays, how much, and why?

---

### 12. Requires Massive Scale to Work
**Symptom**: "Once we have 1 million users, the network effects kick in."

**Why it fails**: Reaching 1M users is extremely hard and expensive. Most startups die before scale.

**Mitigation**: Design for value at 100 users, not 100,000.

---

### 13. Assumes Viral Growth
**Symptom**: "People will just share it because it's so useful."

**Reality**: Organic virality is rare. Most products need paid acquisition.

**Test**: Can you acquire customers profitably through paid channels? If not, you're betting everything on virality (risky).

---

## Founder Red Flags

### 14. Building for Hype, Not Need
**Symptom**: "AI-powered blockchain NFT marketplace for..."

**Why it fails**: Technology-first thinking without solving real problems.

**Fix**: Start with the problem, then choose appropriate technology.

---

### 15. Unwillingness to Pivot
**Symptom**: "This idea is my baby. I'm not changing it."

**Why it fails**: Stubbornness kills startups. Data > ego.

**Mitigation**: Commit to hypotheses, not solutions. Test, learn, adapt.

---

## When Red Flags Are Acceptable

Some red flags can be overcome:
- **Low frequency** → If pain is extreme (e.g., tax software used once/year)
- **Crowded market** → If you have deep domain expertise or unique distribution
- **Behavioral change** → If the benefit is massive (e.g., electric cars)

But **multiple red flags together** are usually fatal.

---

## The Ultimate Red Flag Test

Ask yourself:
1. Would I pay for this *today*?
2. Would I use this *every week*?
3. Would I recommend this to a friend *unprompted*?

If you answered "no" to any question, revisit your assumptions.
